## 1. Company Overview

### 1.1 Who Ren Is
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

Ren, Inc. is a philanthropic fintech company that builds infrastructure and software powering donor-advised funds (DAFs) and charitable giving at scale. Ren operates as the technology and operations layer for financial institutions, wealth management firms, and independent advisors — enabling them to offer white-labeled, fully-featured charitable giving programs to their clients without building or managing the infrastructure themselves.

Ren is not a public-facing charity or foundation. It is a B2B infrastructure provider — the engine underneath the charitable giving programs of major financial institutions.

**Key facts:**
- AUM: $175B+ (updated from earlier $125B+ figure)
- Clients: Financial institutions, RIAs, broker-dealers, wealth management firms
- Non-profit affiliate: **Renaissance Charitable** — Ren's non-profit referral partner vehicle used by WM Firms
- Site-wide tagline: **"Amplify your impact."**

### 1.2 Core Products & Platform
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

**iPhi™ Solution** — Ren's cloud-based DAF management platform. The flagship software product. Pronounced "eye-fee." Enables wealth management firms to offer fully branded DAF programs to their clients. Features include advisor tools, donor portals, grant management, investment options, and reporting.

**RDE (Rapid Disbursement Engine)** — Ren's modern payments and grantmaking infrastructure. Described internally as a "clearinghouse for modern charitable payments." Enables fast, flexible, and reliable grant disbursements. A key differentiator against legacy competitors who rely on check-based grantmaking.

**The Giving Platform** — Ren's unified product suite connecting all stakeholders in the charitable giving ecosystem (donors, advisors, institutions, nonprofits). Tagline: **"One Platform. Every Stakeholder. Infinite Impact."**

### 1.3 Strategic Positioning
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

Ren positions itself as the neutral, best-in-class infrastructure layer — not a competitor to its clients' investment platforms, custodians, or existing relationships. Key positioning phrases (use exactly as written):

- **"On your rails"** — Ren's technology integrates with existing custodial and investment infrastructure rather than replacing it
- **"Custody and investment neutral"** — Ren does not take a position on where assets are held or how they are invested
- **"Engine underneath"** — Ren is the invisible infrastructure powering client-branded programs
- **"Clearinghouse for modern charitable payments"** — Ren's RDE positioning; contrasts with legacy check-based disbursement

## 2. Brand Voice & Tone

### 2.1 Voice Pillars
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

Ren's brand voice is built on four pillars. Every piece of content should reflect at least two of these:

1. **Clarity** — Ren simplifies complexity. Charitable giving infrastructure involves compliance, tax law, multiple stakeholders, and legacy systems. Ren's voice makes this accessible and actionable, not academic or bureaucratic.

2. **Confidence** — Ren speaks with authority about DAFs, grantmaking, and philanthropic technology. The tone is assured, not boastful. Ren has earned its position and speaks from it.

3. **Partnership** — Ren sees its clients (WM firms, advisors, institutions) as partners, not customers. The voice reflects collaborative language: "we work with," "your program," "together."

4. **Purpose** — Ren is in the business of giving. The voice connects infrastructure and technology to real-world philanthropic impact. Features and capabilities are always in service of mission.

### 2.3 Voice: What Ren Sounds Like
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

**Ren sounds like:**
- A knowledgeable partner who understands both financial services and philanthropy
- A builder who has solved hard problems and speaks from experience
- A company that takes giving seriously without being preachy about it
- A modern fintech — precise, efficient, oriented toward outcomes

**Ren does not sound like:**
- A charity asking for donations
- A legacy financial institution (stiff, jargon-heavy, overly formal)
- A startup trying too hard to be disruptive
- A nonprofit with mission fatigue
- Preachy or self-congratulatory about impact

### 2.4 Vocabulary — Use and Avoid
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

**Use:**
- Donor-Advised Fund (first reference), DAF (subsequent references)
- iPhi™ (always with ™ on first use per document)
- RDE or Rapid Disbursement Engine
- Grantmaking (one word)
- Philanthropic giving / charitable giving (interchangeable)
- Wealth management firm / WM Firm
- Financial advisor / advisor (not "financial planner" unless in a specific context)
- Clearinghouse (for RDE positioning)
- Renaissance Charitable (full name on first use; "Renaissance" acceptable after)
- "On your rails" / "engine underneath" (as positioning phrases, not slogans)

**Avoid:**
- "Charity" when referring to Ren itself (Ren is a fintech, not a charity)
- "Donations" (use "contributions" or "gifts" in donor contexts; "assets" in institutional contexts)
- "Customers" (use "clients," "partners," or "firms")
- "Disrupting" or "disruption" (overused; Ren builds, it doesn't disrupt)
- Excessive acronyms without first-use definitions
- "Best-in-class" as a standalone claim (needs support)
- Passive voice in headlines and CTAs

## 3. Messaging Architecture

### 3.1 Core Value Proposition
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

Supporting proof points:
- $175B+ AUM under management on the platform
- End-to-end: from DAF account opening to grant disbursement
- White-label: fully branded for the client institution
- Custody and investment neutral: no conflict with existing relationships
- RDE: modern payments infrastructure for fast, reliable grantmaking
- Compliance and operational support included

### 3.2 Audience-Specific Messaging

#### For Wealth Management Firms (Enterprise Decision-Makers)
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

**Pain points:**
- Clients are moving charitable assets to Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, or Vanguard — away from the advisor relationship
- Building and operating an in-house DAF program is operationally complex and expensive
- Regulatory and compliance burden is significant
- Technology is aging; client experience is poor

**Ren's message:**
> "Keep charitable assets in your ecosystem. Ren gives you a fully branded, compliance-ready DAF program — your name, your clients, your relationship — without the operational burden."

**Key proof points for this audience:**
- White-label: Your brand, not Ren's
- Custody neutral: No disruption to existing custodial relationships
- Operational outsourcing: Ren handles compliance, grantmaking, and technology
- Advisor tools: Advisors can see and manage client charitable accounts alongside investment accounts

#### For Financial Advisors (Individual Practitioners)
🟡 **Confidence: 0.6**

**Pain points:**
- Clients ask about DAFs but advisors don't have a clear offering
- Losing assets under advisement when clients move to a national DAF sponsor
- Difficulty tracking and managing charitable giving alongside investment portfolios
- Uncertainty about DAF best practices and tax optimization

**Ren's message:**
> "Turn charitable conversations into deeper relationships. iPhi gives you the tools to advise, manage, and grow your clients' giving — right alongside their investment portfolios."

**Key proof points for this audience:**
- Integrated advisor dashboard: see charitable and investment assets together
- Proactive giving prompts: appreciated stock, year-end planning
- Client retention: charitable giving deepens the advisor relationship
- Education and support: Ren provides training and resources

#### For Donors / End Clients
🟡 **Confidence: 0.6**

**Pain points:**
- Giving feels fragmented and complex
- Difficult to track impact of charitable gifts
- Want to give strategically but don't know how
- DAFs feel bureaucratic or inaccessible

**Ren's message:**
> "Make giving simple, strategic, and meaningful. Your DAF is a powerful tool — Ren makes it easy to use."

**Key proof points for this audience:**
- Simple account management and grant submission
- Broad nonprofit search and verification
- Tax efficiency and record-keeping built in
- Flexible investment options to grow the giving account

#### For Nonprofits / Grantees
🟡 **Confidence: 0.6**

**Pain points:**
- Slow, unreliable grant disbursements
- Check-based payments that are difficult to track
- Lack of visibility into incoming grants

**Ren's message:**
> "Faster, more reliable grants. RDE means your organization gets paid quickly and accurately — every time."

### 3.3 Competitive Differentiation
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

Ren competes primarily against:
1. **National DAF Sponsors** (Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, Vanguard Charitable) — Ren positions against these by offering white-label, advisor-integrated programs that keep assets in the WM firm's ecosystem
2. **Legacy DAF technology providers** — Ren differentiates on modern technology (iPhi™), RDE payments speed, and full-service operational support
3. **In-house programs** — Ren differentiates on cost, compliance, and time-to-market

**Never name competitors directly in external content** unless in a specific competitive analysis context approved by marketing/legal.

## 7. Content Guidelines by Format

### 7.1 Website Copy
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

- **Headlines:** 5–10 words. Lead with outcome or aspiration. No jargon in H1.
- **Subheads:** 10–20 words. Add specificity to headline.
- **Body:** 2–4 sentences per section. Active voice. Benefit-led.
- **CTAs:** Action verbs. Specific. "See how it works" > "Learn more." "Request a demo" > "Contact us."
- **Stats:** Feature prominently. Use large typography treatment.
- **Tone:** Confident, clean, modern. Avoid adjective overload.

### 7.2 Email (Outbound/Prospecting)
🟡 **Confidence: 0.6**

- **Subject line:** Under 50 characters. Curiosity or direct value. No ALL CAPS.
- **Opening:** Human, specific, contextual (reference their firm or recent news if possible).
- **Body:** 3–5 sentences. One clear point. No feature list.
- **CTA:** One ask only. Low commitment preferred for cold outreach (e.g., "Worth a 15-minute call?").
- **Signature:** Title, direct link (Calendly or similar), clean.
- **Tone:** Peer-to-peer. Not pitch-heavy. Confident but not pushy.

### 7.3 Sales Enablement (One-Pagers, Decks)
🟢 **Confidence: 1.0**

- **Structure:** Problem → Solution → Proof → CTA
- **Headlines:** Bold, outcome-oriented
- **Bullets:** 3 is ideal; max 5. Each bullet = one complete thought.
- **Stats:** Always present; support every major claim
- **Visual hierarchy:** Use Ren's color system. Dark navy for authority; yellow-green for emphasis.
- **Length:** One-pagers stay to one page. Decks: 10–15 slides max for initial presentations.

### 7.4 Thought Leadership (Blog, LinkedIn, Whitepapers)
🟡 **Confidence: 0.6**

- **Angle:** Forward-looking, informed, credible. Ren should have a perspective, not just information.
- **Structure:** Hook → context → insight → implication → CTA (or reflection)
- **Length:** Blog posts: 600–1,200 words. Whitepapers: 1,500–3,000 words.
- **Tone:** Authoritative but accessible. Avoid academic register. Write like a smart practitioner, not a consultant.
- **LinkedIn:** Lead with a hook (first 2 lines visible before "see more"). First-person voice acceptable. 150–300 words ideal for posts.

### 7.5 Advisor-Facing Content
🟡 **Confidence: 0.6**

- **Tone:** Peer-to-peer. Respect advisor expertise; don't over-explain basics.
- **Angle:** Practice management + client retention. Advisors care about relationship depth and AUM retention.
- **Format:** Checklists and scripts perform well. "How to have the DAF conversation with clients" style content.
- **Avoid:** Telling advisors what their clients need. Show, don't prescribe.
